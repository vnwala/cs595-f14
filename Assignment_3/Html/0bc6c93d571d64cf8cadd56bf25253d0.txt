A crime is happening in our schools every day. And it&rsquo;s not the type of crime that hall monitors or security cameras can solve. At issue: Only&nbsp;39 percent of public schools&nbsp;have wireless network access for the whole school. But perhaps the greater offense&mdash;up to this point, at least&mdash;has been apathy.


At work and at home, most of us live our very wired, connected lives&mdash;moving between wi-fi zones as we give little thought to the millions of schoolchildren around the country who go to school every day without Internet or broadband connections, without access to 1:1 computing, and without the benefit of modern handheld learning devices.




&nbsp;
Angry mobs of parents should be storming schools with pitchforks over this critical issue of broadband access, US Department of Education official Richard Culatta told this year&rsquo;s&nbsp;SXSWedu festival. For their part, parents are not, but perhaps there is good reason to believe that the Storming of the Schoolhouse can be thwarted. For now.




&nbsp;
President Barack Obama&rsquo;s&nbsp;ConnectED initiative, announced this summer, aims within five years to connect 99 percent of America&rsquo;s students through next-generation broadband (at speeds no less than 100&nbsp;Mbps and with a target of 1&nbsp;Gbps) and high-speed wireless networks in schools. Now we&rsquo;re talking.
