		<h1 class="entry-title"><a href="http://tomjbeasley.wordpress.com/2014/03/06/bournemouth-rock-aviary/" rel="bookmark">Bournemouth Rock: Mar 6 2014 &#8211; Bournemouth Aviary conservationists leave birds flying&nbsp;high</a></h1>
	</header><!-- .entry-header -->

		<div class="entry-content">
		<p><a href="http://farm4.staticflickr.com/3500/13074094515_0207b40915_b_d.jpg"><img class="alignleft" style="margin-bottom:5px;" alt="Bournemouth Rock - Mar 6 2014 - Bournemouth Aviary conservationists leave birds flying high" src="http://farm4.staticflickr.com/3500/13074094515_0207b40915_b_d.jpg" width="240" height="320" /></a>Tucked away just off Westover Road in Bournemouth’s town centre is a small, wooden building. You hear it before you see it, because it produces a cacophony of birdsong from all corners of the globe.</p>
<p>The Bournemouth Aviary houses donated, rescued and bred bird species as diverse as golden pheasants, common budgerigars and even Amazonian parrots.</p>
<p>Set up by Bournemouth Borough Council more than 50 years ago, the aviary houses a huge variety of birds that are either rescued or bred in captivity. Jane Burch, one of the volunteers helping to run the site, has seen a number of creatures arrive in a very similar way.</p>
<p>“Many people buy birds and, once they’ve got them, they don’t get on with the bird, or there’s some reason why they can’t keep them and then they don’t know what to do with them. Usually, because they’re caring people, they don’t want to just sell them on to somebody who might not take care of them in the way that they wanted, so they give them to us.”</p>

	</header><!-- .entry-header -->

		<div class="entry-content">
		<p><a href="http://farm4.staticflickr.com/3619/13074378644_4e86db753e_b_d.jpg"><img class=" aligncenter" style="margin-bottom:5px;" alt="Bournemouth Rock - Mar 6 2014 - The white noise of TV politics debates" src="http://farm4.staticflickr.com/3619/13074378644_4e86db753e_b_d.jpg" width="640" height="559" /></a>It has been hard to avoid primetime debating programmes recently. The runaway success of Channel 4’s documentary series <a title="Benefits Street website" href="http://www.channel4.com/programmes/benefits-street" target="_blank"><i>Benefits Street</i> </a>has meant that figures like professional contrarian <a title="Katie Hopkins Twitter" href="https://twitter.com/KTHopkins" target="_blank">Katie Hopkins</a> and <i>Guardian</i> columnist <a title="Owen Jones Twitter" href="https://twitter.com/OwenJones84" target="_blank">Owen Jones</a> are eerily omnipresent on our screens. Whether it’s the traditional BBC sparring ground of <i>Question Time</i> or the hyper-active <i>Big Benefits Row: Live</i> on Channel 5, you’re never more than a few moments away from lots of noise about “taxpayers’ money” and “the last Labour government”.</p>
<p>Unfortunately, something has changed about these debates. <i>Question Time </i>has five or six panellists trying to get a word in, but many primetime shows insist on having studios full of people to call White Dee a scrounger.</p>

		<h1 class="entry-title"><a href="http://tomjbeasley.wordpress.com/2014/02/24/nerve-devils-due-review/" rel="bookmark">Nerve Magazine: Feb 14 &#8211; Devil&#8217;s Due&nbsp;review</a></h1>
	</header><!-- .entry-header -->

		<div class="entry-content">
		<p><a href="http://farm8.staticflickr.com/7345/13072708414_06d618c63c_b_d.jpg"><img class=" alignleft" style="margin-bottom:5px;" alt="Nerve Magazine - Feb 14 - Devil's Due review" src="http://farm8.staticflickr.com/7345/13072708414_06d618c63c_b_d.jpg" width="200" height="320" /></a>Unoriginal horror movies are not rare, especially in the found footage sub-genre. However, <em>Devil&#8217;s Due</em> is about as derivative as they come. It tries very hard to be a <em>Rosemary&#8217;s Baby</em> for the found footage generation, but ends up being a bit of a damp squib.</p>
<p>The film deserves credit for its decision to prioritise creeping dread over jump scares, but there&#8217;s a slapdash nature to it all that means the film never quite gels into a cohesive piece of work. Plot explanation is sacrificed to keep the running time lean, but this doesn&#8217;t stop the film feeling flabby and boring in the middle act.</p>
<p>The two central performers bring a whole heap of fun and commitment to their roles, but the found footage gimmick wears thin very early on and the scares are too few and far between for it to be an efficient modern horror.</p>

	</header><!-- .entry-header -->

		<div class="entry-content">
		<p><a href="http://farm8.staticflickr.com/7418/13072494973_e80f5b6e7f_b_d.jpg"><img class=" alignleft" style="margin-bottom:5px;" alt="Nerve Magazine - Feb 14 - Inside Llewyn Davis review" src="http://farm8.staticflickr.com/7418/13072494973_e80f5b6e7f_b_d.jpg" width="600" height="449" /></a>Shamefully snubbed when the Oscar nominations arrived, the latest film from the Coen Brothers is a dingy musical following the disappointing career of a talented folk singer in New York.</p>
<p>The film benefits from its use of extended takes that feature its characters&#8217; musical performances in full. Captured live on set, it creates a raw feel to the movie that really makes it sing, if you&#8217;ll pardon the pun. </p>
<p>Oscar Isaac is a revelation in the lead role, bringing cynicism and despondency to his performance, along with shocking intensity when it is required. A great supporting cast also appears, with quality turns from a cast-against-type Carey Mulligan and a frequently sleeping John Goodman.</p>
<p>Unfortunately, there&#8217;s an aimless wandering to <em>Inside Llewyn Davis</em> that means it never quite feels like a truly great piece of work. Cryptic clues mount up, but the key to unlock the film&#8217;s secrets never materialises.</p>
