                                    <h5 style="height:15px;overflow:hidden"><a href="/marie-et-marion-sacd-cd-anonymous-4/albums/3180933/" class="yellow-link">Marie et Marion [SACD] (CD)</a></h5>
                                    <h4 style="height:15px;overflow:hidden" class="artist"><a href="/music/artist/25639" class="white-link bold">Anonymous 4</a></h4>
                                                                <p style="height:85px;overflow: hidden">
                                     
	The unearthly Anonymous 4 combine historical scholarship with contemporary performance intuition to create their magical sound. Explores songs that juxtapose desire for the earthly lady Marion and adoration of the heavenly Virgin Mary.

                                    <h5 style="height:15px;overflow:hidden"><a href="/human-voice-cd-dntel/albums/3323843/" class="yellow-link">Human Voice (CD)</a></h5>
                                    <h4 style="height:15px;overflow:hidden" class="artist"><a href="/music/artist/150185" class="white-link bold">Dntel</a></h4>
                                                                <p style="height:85px;overflow: hidden">
                                     
	Dntel, solo producer by the name of Jimmy Tamborello has long been creating soundscapes for others to put their human voice over. With&nbsp; Human Voice &nbsp;Tamborello has refused listeners the rights to their own language. Instead, he has created a world where connection is fleeting, melody is deconstructed, and all &ldquo;voices&rdquo; mechanized. An interesting proposition when the bulk of your listeners associate your music with Death Cab For Cutie&rsquo;s emotive crooner Ben Gibbard. Nevertheless, the gambit pays off. Amidst the bits and grids of&nbsp; Human Voice , the mechanized voices morph through layered synths and staccato beats from the unintelligible to a distinct melodic pattern and back again. After 8 tracks It gives the listener the feeling of having communicated with a being not unlike a robot Ben Gibbard.

                                    <h5 style="height:15px;overflow:hidden"><a href="/black-moon-spell-cd-king-tuff/albums/3340377/" class="yellow-link">Black Moon Spell (CD)</a></h5>
                                    <h4 style="height:15px;overflow:hidden" class="artist"><a href="/music/artist/208753" class="white-link bold">King Tuff</a></h4>
                                                                <p style="height:85px;overflow: hidden">
                                     
	When King Tuff released his breakthrough self-titled album in 2012, he came off as a successor to T. Rex&rsquo;s Marc Bolan, alone and stoned in his room and writing perfect power-pop gems. But like Bolan before him, who started with a few English folk albums before going glam, King Tuff aka Kyle Thomas seems to have set his sights higher this time around, filling out the sound with full-bodied heavy rock riffs and pushing his nasal wail into a wolf&rsquo;s howl. Like&nbsp; King Tuff &rsquo;s &ldquo;Anthem,&rdquo; &ldquo;Black Moon Spell&rsquo;s&rdquo; big fat riff sounds pulled out from the ether, like it was always there, judging by the way it nestles into your skull. &ldquo;Rainbow&rsquo;s Run&rdquo; calls to mind another garage-rock luminary, Ty Segall, in the way it takes a simple glam structure and pours acid all over it, impossible distortion and flailing solos flying off the edges. Though there&rsquo;s a winking hair-metal touch to&nbsp; Black Moon Spell , songs like &ldquo;Headbanger&rdquo; (complete with demonic opening) don&rsquo;t stray so far from the glam-garage foundation that it&rsquo;s jarring&mdash;and Thomas&rsquo; voice is too cartoonish and the songs are too damn catchy to really scare off any garage kids, anyway. Even a song called &ldquo;Demon From Hell&rdquo; is more fey and punk than hellish, despite pushing the sound into the red. If there&rsquo;s one thing&nbsp; Black Moon Spell&nbsp; is, it&rsquo;s a great guitar album, as songs like &ldquo;Eyes of the Muse&rdquo; prove, starting with a &rsquo;70s AM radio gold jangle and moving into psychedelic, searching riffs, while Bobby Harlow&rsquo;s production practically places the drums in your living room. There hasn&rsquo;t been a better album released this year to play air guitar and drums to than&nbsp; Black Moon Spell .
